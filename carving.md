# Carving Turns On A Snowboard

## What is carving?

And how is it different from skidding?

A carved turn is one where the snow is only moving along the edge, in parallel to it, whereas in a skidded turn the snow moves across the edge.
Notice how the sides of the board curve inward - the shape of those curves (and the angle of the board against the snow) is what dictates your path in a carved turn.

A skidded turn will often follow this sequence:

1. You rotate your body in the direction of the turn
2. You tip the board slightly on edge
3. Your body keeps rotating, and you cause the board to rotate through the turn

A carved turn, on the other hand:

1. You and the board go straight
2. You tip the board a little bit more, so the edge bites into the snow
3. The board's shape causes it to travel along a curved path
4. The board causes you to rotate through the turn

## How do you know when you're carving?

You'll have a "locked in" feeling during a carve because there isn't much you can do to adjust your path once you commit to the carve. 
You can adjust the edge angle a bit, but your speed and your board's sidecut shape are the dominant factors.

If you carve all of your turns, you can usually see most of them from the chairlift on the way back up, because they carve a v-shaped groove into the snow. 

[This page](https://dmksnowboard.com/how-to-carve-on-a-snowboard-5-steps/) has a great image showing the difference between the track left in the snow by a carved turn vs. the track left by a skidded turn.

## How do you get your carves leaned in further?

Go faster. I can't stress this enough. If you are struggling to get low enough to drag your hip or your elbow, you almost certainly need to go faster.

If you aren't going fast enough, and you lean in further, you'll just fall to the inside. Your body knows this even if your brain does not.

Add some speed, then lean in futher. It'll be scary at first. It takes commitment. But it's really fun.

## What board are good for carving?

It mostly comes down to two things:

Stiffer is better. Under and between your feet, you've got your weight pressing the edge into the snow. 
Ahead of your feet and behind your feet, the amount of force on the edge mostly depends on the stiffness of the board.
If you want more edge hold (and if you're asking this question, you do) then you want a stiffer board, so that the entire edge will be working hard for you - not just the section between your feet.

Also, you probably want something longer - I'm 6'0" and I ride boards in the 170-185 range.

Also, sidecut radius. This is a very deep rabbit-hole and I'm not going to do the subject justice here, but here are some key points:

* If you look at a snowboard from the top, you'll notice that the waist is narrower than the tip and tail.
* For decades the curves on the sides of the board were literally just segments of a circle: arcs with a fixed radius.
* Manufacturers are getting creative with the curves now, but you can still tell a lot about how a board will rides if you know what the average radius is.
* Most off-the-shelf snowboard have a sidecut radius (or average sidecut radius) between 7 and 9 meters.
* If you want to lay down carves, look for a board with a radius in the 12-14 meter range. Look into boardercross boards, for exammple the Nobile N8 in 168cm.
* While it is possible to lay down carves on short-radius board, it's harder. After 4-5 years of trying on 10m boards, I had a much easier time after I switched to a 13m board.
* After even more years of laying down carves on 13m boards, I *can* get laid down on a 10m board... but it's still harder. And less fun - it feels like the entry/carve/exit sequence is over in the blink of an eye.
* A board with a larger sidecut radius will feel awkward at low speeds, but if you increase your speed you'll see what I'm talking about.
* A board with a larger sidecut radius will need more space to complete a turn. If your home mountain's runs are narrow, it might not be the best choice.
* I have a couple boards with sidecuts in the 16-18 meter range, but they need to be ridden very fast before they come alive. And they need a ton of space. It gets awkward.
* Just go a couple meters bigger in your next board, and a couple meters bigger after that. Eventually you'll find the sweet spot for your style, your mountain, and your risk tolerance.
